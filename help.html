Sudoku

How to Play Sudoku

General Rules

  • The classic Sudoku grid is 9x9, divided into 3x3 blocks.
  • Each row, column, and 3x3 block must contain the numbers 1 to 9 exactly once.
  • No duplicate numbers are allowed in any row, column, or block.
  • Some cells are pre-filled as clues; the rest must be filled by the player.
  • There is only one unique solution for each puzzle.

Techniques

  • Brute Force: Systematically trying all possible combinations until a solution is found.
  • Scanning/Cross-Hatching: scanning rows, columns, and block for a specific digit
  • Last Free Cell/Hidden Single: When 8 out of 9 cells in a row, column, or 3x3 block are filled, the single remaining empty cell must contain the missing number.
  • Naked Single: When a cell has only one possible candidate number remaining after checking its row, column, and block.
  • Naked Pairs/Triples: If two cells in a row/column/box contain only the same two candidates, those numbers can be eliminated from other cells in that unit.
  • Hidden Pairs/Triples: When two or three numbers are restricted to the same two or three cells within a unit, even if those cells have other candidates.
  • Pointing Pairs/Triples: If a candidate within a 3x3 block only appears in a single row or column, that candidate can be removed from the rest of that row or column outside the block.
  • Locked Candidates: When a candidate number in a row or column is restricted to a specific 3x3 box, it can be eliminated from other cells in that box
  • X-Wing: A pattern involving two rows and two columns, where a number is restricted to two possible cells, allowing elimination of that number from the columns/rows involved.
  • Swordfish: An extension of X-Wing using three rows and three columns to identify and eliminate candidate numbers.
  • XY-Wing/W-Wing: Complex, chained logical, and "almost locked" sets to force eliminations in difficult puzzles
